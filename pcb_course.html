PCB Designing – Introduction

Start your journey into professional circuit board design

🔍 What is a PCB?

A PCB (Printed Circuit Board) is a flat, usually green-colored board made from a non-conductive material. It has thin copper lines (called traces) that work like roads to connect electronic components together.

Instead of using many loose wires, a PCB helps keep everything neat and compact. It acts as the foundation (or backbone) for almost every electronic device—from simple LED blinkers to advanced robots and computers.

PCBs are used in everything from smartphones and TVs to cars, drones, medical tools, and satellites. They make devices more reliable, faster, and easier to fix or upgrade.

📂 Types of PCBs

🔸 Single-Sided PCB:
This is the simplest type of PCB, where copper is placed on just one side of the board. It’s commonly used in basic circuits like LED blinkers, small battery chargers, and toys. They're affordable and easy to make — great for beginners!

🔸 Double-Sided PCB:
These have copper on both the top and bottom sides. Small holes (called vias) connect both layers. You’ll find these in audio systems, power supply units, and industrial control panels — ideal when you need a bit more complexity.

🔸 Multi-Layer PCB:
Think of this like a PCB sandwich! It has three or more layers stacked together, allowing for compact and powerful designs. These are used in smartphones, laptops, aerospace systems, and high-speed internet routers. They’re great when space is tight and performance matters.

🔸 Rigid PCB:
As the name says, these boards are stiff and don’t bend. They are found in everyday items like desktops, TVs, washing machines, and car dashboards. They offer long life and stable support for mounted components.

🔸 Flexible PCB (Flex PCB):
Made from bendable material, these PCBs can twist and fold! You’ll find them inside smartwatches, foldable phones, fitness bands, and medical sensors. They save space and are great for devices that move or flex.

🔸 Rigid-Flex PCB:
A clever mix of rigid and flexible sections. These are used where parts of the circuit must bend while others stay fixed — like in digital cameras, aerospace instruments, or surgical tools. They reduce the number of connectors and cables, making the device more reliable.

📐 Layers & Copper Tracks

A PCB is like a multi-story building. It has different **layers**, and each one plays a special role in helping the circuit work smoothly:

  • 🔹 Top Layer: This is like the roof — it holds all the **main components** like resistors, microcontrollers, and sensors. These are usually **surface-mount** parts placed directly on this top side.
  • 🔹 Inner Layers: Imagine secret floors between the top and bottom — they carry important electrical paths like **power** and **data signals**. These layers exist only in **multi-layer PCBs** and help keep things neat and compact.
  • 🔹 Bottom Layer: This is the base of the board, often used for extra wiring and **through-hole components** (those with pins that go through the board).

Now let’s talk about the highways of a PCB — the copper tracks (also called traces). These tracks connect components and allow electricity to flow between them — just like roads connect cities.

When designing copper tracks, we consider:

  • Width of the track — wider for more current
  • Spacing between tracks — to prevent short circuits
  • Signal speed — important for high-speed communication

Some PCBs also include large areas called power planes and ground planes. These work like giant expressways — they help deliver power and reduce electrical noise, keeping the circuit clean and stable.

🧾 Gerber Files Explained

**Gerber files** are like the blueprint files for your PCB. They’re generated from your PCB design software (like EasyEDA or KiCad) and tell the manufacturer exactly how to build your board — layer by layer.

Just like an architect provides drawings to a builder, **you provide Gerber files to a PCB manufacturer** (like JLCPCB or PCBWay) to create your physical board. Without these files, your design stays stuck on the screen — it can’t be fabricated.

Each Gerber file describes a specific part of your PCB. Here’s what’s usually included:

  • Top & Bottom Copper Layers: These show where all the electrical connections (tracks) go.
  • Solder Mask: These define where the green (or any color) coating goes to protect the copper and leave openings for pads.
  • Silkscreen: This is where the text and component labels are printed (usually white).
  • Board Outline: Defines the shape and size of your PCB — its boundary.
  • Drill Files: Tell the machine where to make holes for components like resistors or IC sockets.

Once your design is ready, you export all these files from your software as a **.zip file** and upload it to the manufacturer’s website. They use it to create your custom PCB exactly as you designed.

⚙️ Essential PCB Layout Shortcuts

When you're designing a PCB, using keyboard shortcuts can save you a lot of time and make your workflow smoother. Here are some handy shortcuts that are commonly used in tools like EasyEDA or KiCad:

  • Ctrl + R: Rotate the selected component (helps you position parts easily).
  • Del or Backspace: Quickly delete a component or wire you no longer need.
  • W: Start drawing a wire or trace to connect components.
  • Ctrl + Z / Ctrl + Y: Undo or redo your last action—super helpful when you make a mistake.
  • Ctrl + S: Save your design progress (do this often to avoid losing your work).
  • Ctrl + E: Export your Gerber files when your design is ready for manufacturing.

Mastering these shortcuts will make you faster and more confident as you build your PCB projects!
